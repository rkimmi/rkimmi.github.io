Connecting

Have you tried using any of the insight and techniques for growing trust and sincerity? How did it go?

All (good) relationships are dependant on trust. This becomes obvious when trying to build relationships, but its maintenance can be more easily overlooked. This chapter made me think more about maintaining good relationships through trust, but I have not gone out of my way to test or practice the proposed techniques.

Try empathic listening - how did it go?

Mindful and empathic listening are things I practice more often now, mostly only during informal conversation. It is usually triggered by realisations that I may be talking too much, and so decided to step back and focus on listening to the other person. This is a good habit.

How did you find the loving kindness meditation?

The loving kindness meditation can alter your negative perceptions of a person. It was challenging holding onto feelings of love and kindness while visualising someone I did not like, but found that making the effort was beneficial in releasing negative feelings toward that person.

Complete the political awareness exercise here in writing:

  1. Think of a difficult situation from your present or past, when there was some conflict or disagreement, something real, something that has some meaning and potency for you.

    2. The month is September, a very busy time for students in their last semester of an undergraduate art degree. Official art history and theory lectures have been replaced at this time with less formal lectures hosted by guests from outside of the university, with varying successes and experiences in the art world network. Only one tutor has been tasked with conducting and organising this course. They have done a really great job, getting a diverse group of interesting people to talk weekly to students about their working-world experience. The last lecture will be given by a somewhat recent graduate and daughter of another tutor. Their father is overwhelmingly proud of them, and is definitely not missing their academic debut. The potential hundreds of other recently graduated, practicing artists who would be thrilled for this opportunity are not contacted, as this is more convienient.

  2. Describe the situation as though you are 100 percent correct and reasonable. Do that either in writing or by talking about it in a monologue.

    3. I can't believe I'm going to sit through this, everyone else is boycotting this lecture and now I fully understand why. A ten minute late start, we've never waited this long on account of not everyone having arrived yet. The speaker spends these ten minutes talking to my tutor openly about another artist's work which they describe as 'trendy', as in not authentic or something. They assume no one else in this room knows who they are speaking of. The artist in question is my friend and I am getting upset, this is trash and unprofessional.

    The power-point consists of their miraculously linear progression from art school; 'heres some work I made in first, second and third year'. I hate this kind of painting. 'Then I went to New York less than a summer later'. Her father continuously interjects with his own stories about their trip. He's a tutor and this is crossing way too many boundaries. Other students are looking on with fear and inadequecy, some of us are stuggling to afford to eat and I very much doubt any of us are making it to New York.

    The lecture continues with 'then I did this show which my father organised', 'then we went to Venice and other parts of Europe because Documenta, and the Venice Biennale coincide this year and we really recommend going in 2027 when they are in alignment again', and then 'I got a studio which I charge other artists exorbitant rent for'.

    After the lecture, their father thanks me for attending, but then retracts with 'I shouldn't be thanking you, you had to come because it was compulsary.' This is gross. A friend and myself put together an email outlining our discomforts and the overall hostility of the experience; I am exhausted, already dealing with the university on other accounts and now have to face this head-on in an ethics meeting.

  3. Now describe the situation as though the other person is (or the other people are) 100 percent correct and reasonable. Do that either in writing or by talking about it in a monologue.

    4. Tutor A: I have had to depend on my own resources and contacts to put together this course with not much help from the university. Resources are stretched and this makes it harder to do my job, for example; late-notice cancellations are just one of the things I solely had to troubleshoot during this programme. I may very be feeling quite ready for this course to be over. This is the finale lecture, so thought student's could hear from someone closer to their situation. I know this speaker quite well and can trust that they will commit their time. I've asked them to speak about their experiences post-art school, but these are only loose guidelines. It's not required that we approve or check the lecture content beforehand.

    Speaker: I'm excited to be offered this position to speak to students and assume a role-model position. Assuming these students want the same successes as myself, I will share them and offer advice. Having the support of my father and Tutor A feels great. Tutor A is a family friend, but is also very successful, so making our closeness known makes me too feel important. There are family expectations that I do well in painting, it is assumed that my talents are a birth-right and so I worry that others may out perform me. I may be feeling defensive as some people assume I have not had to work as hard as others for my success. These insecurities may manifest in me denying my privilege and belittling others to regain feelings of power. I may not be accurately portraying myself in this setting because of these feelings.
