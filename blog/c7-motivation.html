Motivation

How does life look through the 'pleasure, passion and purpose' lens?

Pleasure as motivation is not sustainable. The prerequisite for success discussed in Chapter 6; failure, is not very often a source of pleasure. Passion works well, and is necessary, but its motivational power does not overcome that of purpose. Purpose here refers to a source of motivation which is larger and more important than yourself.

Friends and peers, including myself who are invested in making things seem to ongoingly discuss this kind of purpose; 'why do you (insert 'make art', 'write', 'code', 'other thing' here) / and 'who do you do it for?'. While making work for yourself is awesome, in the past I had fallen despondent when realising some work didn't go further than offering me a temporary self-satisfaction. Long-term, sustained practice comes out of purpose; making work / doing things for yourself — and others.

From the 'discovering values and higher purpose' exercise:

    My core values are:

  • individual personal power and autonomy.

    • I stand for:

  • denouncing exploitation and abuse/s of power, at institutional and indiviudal levels.
  • radical change in education, the justice system and economic inequality.

    • How did you find the resilience ideas and meditation exercise?

    I stuggled a lot with the meditation exercise. None of my recent failures or successes had been polarising; failures were followed by small successes, and no successes went exactly to plan — a failure, perhaps? The chapter cleared this up after with the resilience ideas, that success and failure live closely together. My response was something like: well, obviously.
